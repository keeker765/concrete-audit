🔍 Can AI detect concrete specimen fraud on construction sites?

In China's construction industry, concrete test blocks must be sampled on-site during casting (wet state) and sent to a lab for strength testing weeks later (dry state). The problem: there's a window where blocks can be swapped — replacing a substandard specimen with a stronger one before lab submission.

I built a computer vision system to automatically detect this.

---

💡 Core idea

Every concrete surface has a unique texture fingerprint. By extracting and matching local features between the wet-state and dry-state photos of the same block, the system can determine whether they came from the same physical specimen.

---

🔬 Technical approach

📌 Preprocessing pipeline
→ QR code recognition to pair wet/dry photos by specimen ID
→ SAM (Segment Anything Model) to isolate the concrete face as ROI
→ Blue label detection + perspective correction to normalize viewing angle
→ Rotation alignment (0°/90°/180°/270°) to handle different photo orientations

📌 Feature matching
→ Primary: SuperPoint + LightGlue (deep learned sparse matching)
→ Baselines: ALIKED, SIFT, DoGHardNet, LoFTR, RoMa
→ Score = 0.5 × mean confidence + 0.5 × RANSAC inlier ratio
→ Score ≥ 0.60 → SAME | Score < 0.60 → DIFFERENT

---

📊 Results (66 specimen pairs)

SuperPoint + LightGlue consistently achieved the best discrimination between genuine and swapped specimens. The main challenge was false positives caused by adjacent blocks appearing in the wet-state photo — a data collection issue that highlights how much real-world messiness matters.

---

🧩 What I learned

The hardest part wasn't the algorithm — it was the data.

Real construction site photos are inconsistent: varying lighting, angles, blurry images, partially cropped frames, label tabs extending outside the mask area, and molds containing multiple adjacent blocks. None of this shows up in clean benchmark datasets.

Building a robust preprocessing pipeline to handle these edge cases was easily 70% of the work.
